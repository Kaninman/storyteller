The stories of cultures both supernatural and mundane are filled with the idea of a long-lost Golden Age whose grace we have fallen from. Mages and mortals both believe in infinitely wise Atlantis. The Uratha long for the time before Father Wolf’s fall. Christians imagine life before Original Sin. The Nameless Empire the Mummies hearken from was an archaeological fact, but it never had the glory and power they now invest in it. And even now the idea of a beautiful Tartarian Empire which held secrets of beauty that we are now denied existed a mere generation ago gains traction among those beaten down by the mundane ugliness of modernity. All of them look around at the world, at what a miserable mess it is, and think “It can’t have always been like this. This isn’t the best of all possible worlds. I know we could do better—we must have lost that.”

The best of all possible worlds is what we, the Hopeful, refer to as “the Fearless Kingdoms.”

It wasn’t “perfect” because nothing can be. There was anger, hatred, and sorrow; they are a part of us. If there is freedom, if we have independent minds and fates, then we must have the freedom to screw up, even very badly. We can’t have joy without sorrow… but we don’t need nearly this much sorrow, and the Fearless Kingdoms found that balance. There was strife, but not nearly so much or so deep. All parties involved knew that ultimately their opponents were human and wanted to settle their differences with as little conflict as possible. There were forces of evil, even fiendish monsters from the dark, but against heroic champions evil never even got a foothold. Even so, people died. When they died too young, their [[Ghosts|ghost]] would return for a while, say its goodbyes, and finish its business. If you thought your life wasn’t well-lived, you reincarnated and got another go at it, perhaps multiple times, until you could look back and say “Yeah, I’m happy with that.”

“The Fearless Kingdoms” wasn’t the name back then, of course, it was just “the world” or “reality” or “Earth.” It wasn’t a unified governing kingdom; different people with different ideas and needs were able to determine their own fates. Some of them had monarchs, but not all; there were democracies and anarchists who lived their own lives too. Yet the name isn’t inaccurate, because no matter how they were governed, these Kingdoms all had Nobility.

There were leaders, wise and just and empathetic, whether they ruled by edict or example. Leaders the people could genuinely trust, both in their good intentions and their skill. Leaders with truly noble souls to believe in and rally around. Absolute monarchs, elected politicians, informal figures of wisdom, even parental figures; they all had Kings and Queens, Princes and Princesses. The Noble were the ones you could trust because they loved you as much as you should love yourself.

Finally, magic was not a thing we HAD, magic was a thing we WERE. Magic was the expression of our humanity, our ability to make things better and keep growing upwards—the Light of Hope, the ability to see a better future and make it come to pass. Some would find the love and kindness and humanity inside them just couldn’t be contained and it would surge out. You would Blossom, and realize your destiny as a shining beacon of Light, a bearer of Hope: As a Noble, as Princes and Princesses given to magic as birds are given to song.

And as a Noble of the Fearless Kingdoms, you would be utterly without fear. Because fear, as we know it, didn’t exist. People could feel caution, or wariness, or concern for their safety; it’s not like they were blind or suicidal. But fear? An emotion that doesn’t just tell you about danger, but paralyzes you from doing anything about it and then tortures you until you give in and do something you know will hurt yourself? Absolutely incomprehensible.
