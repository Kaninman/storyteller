# Dual Identity

A Princess’s Transformation into her perfected self is not an illusion or disguise, it is a different identity. Simple Perception rolls cannot link the two identities, any more than you could make a sufficient Perception roll to determine that guy in the mailroom who looks kind of like Elvis Presley is secretly, somehow, the real Elvis Presley. Similarly, no simple Subterfuge or Empathy roll can determine a Princess is lying when asked about having a dual identity, and lying to cover your identity is never a Compromise.

But there’s more. A Princess’s Dual Identity is an inherent power that provides incredible magical concealment. It doesn’t stop others from seeing the Princess’s nature like a Changeling’s Mask would. It doesn’t stop others from remembering a Princess like a Werewolf’s Lunacy would. The magic protects a much narrower bit of information and protects it much more strongly.

Any attempt to use magic or supernatural ability to link a Princess’s two identities provides no useful information. The caster doesn’t know it failed, the ability goes off, but the information they get is useless. The supernatural ability provides no useful information even by omission:

if you telepathically scan a crowd to see who is thinking about secretly being a Princess, you can’t see that there’s one person blocking your scan and determine they have magical protection. They appear to be as normal as everyone else. The sympathetic connections between the two identities aren’t unnaturally severed, they’re as weak as you would expect two people who have been to the same location at different times to be. You can’t magically track where a Princess walked from and then scry into the past to see someone transform into her; you won’t see that your vision is blocked, she just appears to come out of nowhere. Dual Identity doesn’t negate the magic used against you, it prevents that magic from gathering useful information that would link your two identities.

Which is extremely strange when you think about it, right? It’s as bulletproof an anti-magic protection as they come, and that would be remarkable even if that protection wasn’t something that seems to be the diametric opposite of what the Transformation does: expressing your true self for the world to see. It’s like you took all the magical oomph of one of those magical thingies that keeps other supernaturals secret, and focused it into a super narrow band like putting your thumb on the mouth of a hose to make the spray more powerful. There’s no way that’s natural, and yet Dual Identity seems to come bundled with the Blossoming. Somebody definitely had to have created some kind of enchantment on purpose… and if the Radiant knew who it was, that would be real helpful. The best guesses right now are that the Queen of Tears worked some great magic that protects her agents, or the Queen of Mirrors did it in a flurry of denial about the self, but neither will say anything about it.

## Dual Identity Protections

While Dual Identity’s magical protection isn’t perfect, it’s damn near close. There’s only really two circumstances where magic can link two identities. First, if a magic user has a Princess’s Phylactery, they can gather information by omission, meaning they know what’s being blocked and can quickly figure out who their target is by process of elimination. Second, if a magic user already gathered enough information about the Princess that the magical scrying is simply confirming their existing theory, then Dual Identity can’t throw them off—it’ll confirm their suspicions.

That’s the thing – Dual Identity protects you from magic linking your two identities, and it protects you from the most casual sort of observation, but it does nothing to stop good old-fashioned mundane deduction. An Investigation that is able to find mundane clues will be able to uncover the secret, if there’s enough evidence to believe a conclusion as wild as “that guy in the mailroom really is Elvis.”

Unless players have been extremely careless, the Storyteller shouldn’t have a supernatural antagonist follow them back to their mundane lives. Very few supernatural antagonists are even aware that Princesses have mundane lives and won’t even try to look; if they do, they will most likely use magic and not be aware it’s giving them useless junk data. There might be some other ways to pierce the Dual Identity’s protections, but they aren’t a matter of throwing more dice at the problem. Rather, they will come down to some kind of interaction with the Princess herself. Any clue a supernatural antagonist gets should be directly caused by some major error the player made, and there should always be a warning that something is happening:
people start asking questions, there are mysterious vans parked where she often Transforms, someone interviews her as a witness to an event she was secretly a part of. This gives players the chance to destroy or cast doubt on the clues.

A careful balance has to be struck. A Princess can’t be careless and cavalier with their dual identity, they shouldn’t be able to do anything they want and then just clean it up at the last second. But the Princess’s mundane relationships are incredibly important thematically and mechanically and the best course of action should never be “cut off everyone so I don’t put them in danger.” There are things a character could do to make the people they care about safer, but they’re tedious and get in the way of playing the actual game, so you don’t want to do those either. In other areas of the game it is very possible to do everything right and still fail, but if a mundane relationship is put in danger, the player should always be able to say “Okay, that’s fair, that’s on me, I messed that up.”

It’s more appropriate to have minor mundane antagonists come close to figuring out the Princess’s identity, but who can’t pose a major threat and don’t grasp the scope of what it fully means. This is appropriate to the genre—you don’t worry about the police tracking you, you worry about that weirdo classmate who always has his eye on you—and makes sense that someone who is working on a much pettier level will focus more on petty things like “why do you always excuse yourself to the bathroom when a monster shows up,” because they don’t have the resources to look for something bigger than that.
