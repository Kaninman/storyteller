# Alhambra

[[Campaign- City of Sin/Locations/The Dreamlands & Dark World/Twilight Kingdoms/Alhambra|Alhambra]] is the Last City, the only remaining part of [[The Fearless Kingdoms]]. Perpetually gloomy under a sunless sky, it is lit by lanterns burning stolen hope, flickering through the constant rain. Depression is so thick in the atmosphere you can almost drink it. It is the stronghold of [[the Queen of Tears]] and her followers, who some consider the Radiant’s most important enemies; the Radiant oppose their goals and operations at every turn. Its gates link to locations all over the world where Ravens steal hope, support Nightmares, and undermine everything the Ra-
diant believe in.

And it is open to all the Radiant at all times. It is a sanctuary and neutral ground for all, where even the Queen’s enemies can taste an echo of the generosity that once defined her life. If any Princess needs something in Alhambra, Alhambra will offer it – if it’s possible without compromising its own operations.

None of [[The Twilight Queens]] believe themselves to be the enemies of the Radiant. They believe the Radiant just need time and more information to see that the right choice is to join their cause. The Queen of Tears feels the weight of sorrow and hopelessness on her heart, and knows it is inevitable that the five [[The Queens|Radiant Queens]] will see
the truth and join her. Maybe they will even bring some sliver of joy to Alhambra, though the Queen knows better than to invest her heart into that kind of hope. Opposition from the Radiant is just a temporary condition. The Queen of Tears will use Alhambra and the comforts it offers to ensure that the Radiant don’t cause too much damage to its operations before they wise up and join.

While pursuing her own goals is important, the Queen offers up Alhambra so the Radiant won’t get killed. They’re committed to doing suicidally stupid things out of a misguided sense of hope, but each death is still a tragedy. Each death of a Princess that could have been avoided if the Ravens had convinced them to join adds another ounce to the weight on her heart.

# The City

Alhambra sits somewhere in the Dark World, but absolutely nothing lies beyond its borders and gates. It is a city unto itself. Nearly every square foot of it is filled with cobblestone roads, marble courtyards, Roman-concrete canals, endless structures of brick, granite, wrought iron. Despite the city’s age, and the inability to expand its borders, the streets and public buildings all give the sense of being too large, too empty. The people walk the streets with gloomy eyes, pulling their coats tighter, lost in the dungeons of their own thoughts.

No sun or stars hang above Alhambra, but if they did, they’d be rarely visible through the omnipresent clouds. When it isn’t raining in Alhambra, it’s about to rain. The rain is clean and drinkable, though ever so slightly salty, like human tears; all of the architecture features gutters and drains to redirect the constant flow of rain to the canals that run alongside major thoroughfares.

The most important physical feature of Alhambra is the lamps. On posts and in sconces, gas-burning lamps are omnipresent, casting greenish or bluish illumination as they burn stolen hope. Mundane sources of illumination seem weak and miniscule by comparison. Here, the lanterns are all that matter. They’re designed to be operable by a toddler and starting at age 7, many Alhambrans serve in the Lamplighter Corps, wearing midnight-blue sashes and dutifully refilling lamps one by one.

The presence of the lanterns is what keeps the [[All-Consuming Darkness]] out; it’s why Alhambra is the only physical place that has no “All-Consuming Darkness” Tension Condition (p. 274).

It isn’t the literal illumination of the lanterns that keeps the Darkness out, of course, it’s the hope they burn to create that light. If you go to sleep in your Alhambran bedroom with the lights off, it’s not like Darkspawn will mani- fest in your bedroom to attack.

Alhambrans still won’t sleep with the lights off, though.

The palace of the Queen of Tears is a tower that looms above the city, tall enough for the upper reaches to vanish into the clouds. Its stained-glass windows used to depict moments of Calat’s glorious history, but they’ve been corroded, broken and rebuilt so many times they are now just abstract groupings of muted colors. The walls’ once-vibrant murals painted by the city’s great artists are now almost entirely washed away by rain. The tip of the tower is the only thing that extends above the cloud cover, and only the Queen of Tears herself is allowed at the highest level. She thinks about jumping. She doesn’t know if it’s courage or cowardice stopping her.

# The People

Everyone living in Alhambra is depressed.

They know it, and they don’t love it—they are depressed, after all—but they accept it. The Queen’s heart echoes to all of them and carries their hearts with it, but this isn’t a magical compulsion any more than a buzzkill or a melancholy story is magical. They are depressed, they say, because it’s an accurate assessment of the world. Depression is welcome to numb how awful their emotions would feel,
and it lets them see the world without deluding themselves about their own capabilities.

The Radiant are the first real “guests” the city has had in its denizens’ lifetimes, after Storms and Mirrors were written off long ago. Everyone else who came to the City intended to stay or already followed the Queen of Tears. If one has a scheduled meeting with an Alhambra-dweller, they
appear perfectly clean, crisp, dapper, and presentable. Meet them a few days later, and they will be wearing the same clothes, their hair messier and unwashed, gunk ac- cumulating under their fingernails. They don’t take care of
themselves unless someone prods them into it. There isn’t much point to them in showering and changing clothes every day even with modern amenities making that possible. They sleep too much or too little, and dark rings lie under every weary eye.

And yet there is a dignity to them and their sorrow. Their lives are worth living, but if one decides that theirs isn’t, suicide is considered a fundamental right of all living things. Someone who exercises it will be seen off in a bittersweet parting ceremony. Their emotional highs are not high, they are realistic. Alhambrans generally don’t grin, but a weary little half-smile, the chuckle of laughter that escape their mouths when something is too funny to deny, those mean more than anything outside the City, where
joy is all falsehood, delusion, and denial. Their feelings are like their flowers: slimmer than those outside , frailer, with less light and no Sun to reach toward, but still alive, with the normally vivid colors faded into beautiful and understated pastels.

An Alhambran feels sorrow over the state of the world, for it is so vast and Alhambra is so small, yet must fit everything good within it. They feel sorrow that Calat and all the Fearless Kingdoms can never come back. They don’t generally feel sorrow or regret over their light sources. The lamps are precious to them, and to be concerned over the fact they burn hope from the outside world is like being concerned that a sunflower is sucking up too much light and weakening the Sun. The outside world is one of utter and rightful despair. Dreams are crushed, friends betrayed, hope is shattered every day. Better that some of it be collected and put to use keeping the Last City alive. Anything that matters is kept here.

The Princesses who keep the lights burning are known as Ravens—at least half of all Princesses born in Alhambra take the name “Raven” for their alternate identity—and are treated with great respect and deference. While it isn’t illegal to offend or speak ill of them, it is considered incredibly rude and disrespectful of the Queen herself. Ravens are expected to be given first choice of City amenities or luxuries. Commoners are not required to kneel in their presence, but a respectful bow or salute is considered polite.
